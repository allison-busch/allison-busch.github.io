About Allison Busch

Allison Busch (1969-2019) was Associate Professor of Hindi and Indian Literature in the Department of Middle Eastern South Asian, and African Studies at Columbia University. She received her BA from University of British Columbia in 1992, and her Ph.D. in Hindi literature from the University of Chicago in 2003. From 2001 to 2005, she taught at University of North Carolina-Chapel Hill. In 2005, she joined Columbia as Assistant Professor in Hindi. She was promoted to Associate Professor in 2011.

Through her groundbreaking work on classical Hindi literature, she awoke a fresh appreciation for aesthetic experiences that had long been devalued. She shared her superb knowledge of the history and politics, as well as the music, wit, beauty, and pathos of classical Hindi literature in her lectures and in numerous publications. Her Poetry of King: The Classical Hindi Literature of Mughal India (Oxford University Press, 2011) drew on a wide array of published and unpublished texts and commentaries to show us how the incredibly rich courtly style of Hindi literature sustained traditional Indian political and aesthetic ideals in the Mughal and Rajput worlds of the seventeenth through nineteenth centuries. Her articles were immensely influential: "Braj Beyond Braj: Classical Hindi in the Mughal World"(2009), for example, and "Hidden in Plain View: Brajbhasha Poets at the Mughal Court" (2010), reshaped the scholarly conversation about early modern literary cultures broadly conceived. In the scholarly world of classical Hindi poetry, she had acquired a legendary status among senior and emerging scholars, who looked to her for always learning something new about the art of translating and reciting verse and song.

At the time of her death in October, 2019, she was completing three book manuscripts, each about a different aspect of early-modern Hindi literature: a study of Rajput martial poetry (with William Pinch and Dalpat Rajpurohit); a wide-ranging account of the vernacular histories of Mughal India; a monograph, co-authored with the art historian Molly Aitken, about Indian love and the ideal Hindu heroine among Mughal connoisseurs, and, in particular, the powerful effects made possible when poetry and painting come together. Professor Busch was also working on several new translations, including a translation of Keshavdas's 1591 Rasikapriya.
